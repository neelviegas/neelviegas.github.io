01

Thrust Analysis Tool

McGill Rocket Team
Role: Injector team lead Stack: Python · Matplotlib · NumPy Timeline: May 2026 – present

I lead this project as the lead of the injector team on the McGill Rocket Team. The goal was to extract the coefficient of combustion efficiency from our hotfire data.

I built the tool in Python using Matplotlib. It ingests raw test-stand telemetry from a hybrid hotfire and turns it into a clean, four-panel analysis: oxidizer mass flow rate (via the Dyer model), run vs. chamber pressure with the pressure differential that drives flow, run temperature, and cumulative oxidizer mass consumed.

Handling the messy realities of test data — like substituting a faulted thermocouple (tc4 in for tc5) — was part of making the output trustworthy enough to feed downstream efficiency calculations.

Result — HF1.1
Total oxidizer 11.70 kg · peak 1.86 kg/s · average 1.69 kg/s.
02

Vortex Injector Redesign

McGill Rocket Team
Role: Injector project (member → co-lead) Stack: Siemens NX · GD&T Timeline: Aug 2025 – May 2026

In response to a growing body of research advocating for vortex injector hole angles closer to 45 degrees, I was tasked with updating our bench-scale and full-scale vortex injectors to optimize oxidizer–fuel mixing in our hybrid rocket propulsion system.

I presented my research to the team, then redesigned both injectors in NX and produced new manufacturing drawings — including the full GD&T, sections, and detail views needed to actually make the parts.

  • Researched and justified the ~45° hole-angle change against prior geometry.
  • Rebuilt the bench-scale and full-scale injector CAD in Siemens NX.
  • Authored new drawings with tolerances, seal callouts, and detail scales.
03

Automatic Curtains

Personal Project
Role: Solo — design, CAD, circuit, code Stack: SolidWorks · Arduino UNO · 3D print Timeline: Jun 2026 – present

I live next to a building whose lights, unfortunately for me, never turn off. That said, I like waking up to the sun without compromising my sleep. This led me to build automatic curtains.

I designed the circuit and wrote a script on an Arduino UNO that closes my curtains at night and opens them in the morning. Then I designed a gear train and supports for the circuit and the servo in SolidWorks. I assembled everything — and can now sleep in peace.

  • Designed the drive gears and servo/electronics mounts in SolidWorks, then 3D printed them.
  • Built the circuit around an Arduino UNO with a timed open/close routine.
  • Iterated on the gear set until the torque and fit were right.
04

Local AI Model

Personal Project
Role: Solo Stack: Python · Open-source LLMs · RAG Timeline: Jun 2026 – present

Inspired by work I did at Pratt & Whitney, I built a locally-run retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system using open-source AI models.

The focus was tuning the parameters for an ideal pipeline: indexing → retrieval → augmentation & generation — so that a fully local model could answer questions accurately over a private document set.

  • Ran open-source models entirely locally (no external API).
  • Tested indexing and retrieval parameters for accuracy and speed.
  • Tuned the augmentation + generation stage for grounded answers.
05

Chair with Assisted Seating & Standing

MECH 292
Role: CAD (solo) · circuit & presentation (team of 5) Stack: Siemens NX · Circuit Timeline: Jan 2026 – May 2026

This was my first ever time CADing in NX — for MECH 292, our design class, in a team of five.

The chair uses an assist mechanism to help a user sit down and stand up. I CADed the entire assembly on my own, and the team designed and assembled a supporting circuit. We presented the project to the class and answered design questions.

  • Modelled the full chair assembly in Siemens NX (solo).
  • Contributed to the assist-mechanism circuit (team).
  • Presented and defended the design to the class (team).
